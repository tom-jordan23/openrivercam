# Comprehensive Research Report: River Flow Monitoring in Humanitarian Contexts

**Prepared for:** OpenRiverCam Project
**Date:** November 18, 2025
**Focus:** Positioning camera-based river flow measurement systems for humanitarian use by non-technical staff

---

## Executive Summary

**Key Findings:**

1. **Critical Need**: River flow monitoring is increasingly vital in humanitarian contexts, with flood early warning systems proven to reduce fatalities by 43% and economic losses by 35-50% in developing countries.

2. **Technology Gap**: While sophisticated satellite and modeling systems exist, there is a significant gap for accessible, low-cost, field-deployable systems that can be operated by non-technical humanitarian staff.

3. **Deployment Barriers**: Humanitarian organizations face substantial challenges deploying technical monitoring systems, including limited resources, poor infrastructure, power/connectivity constraints, and lack of technical capacity.

4. **Documentation Imperative**: Successful humanitarian technology adoption requires documentation that eliminates jargon, uses visual aids extensively, provides step-by-step guidance, and is tested with non-technical users.

5. **Proven Models**: Community-based early warning systems in Nepal, Bangladesh, and Uganda demonstrate that locally-operated, simple technology can be highly effective when properly designed and supported.

## 1. River Flow Monitoring and Hydrology in Humanitarian Contexts

### 1.1 Overview of Applications

River flow monitoring serves multiple critical functions in humanitarian operations:

**Flood Early Warning Systems (FEWS)**
- Community-based flood early warning systems (CBFEWS) provide near real-time warnings when rising flood waters are detected, empowering communities to act with sufficient time to reduce injury, loss of life, and property damage [ICIMOD, 2024].
- Early warning systems have proven effectiveness: properly designed systems reduce flood-related fatalities by up to 43% and economic losses by 35-50% [UNDP, 2024].
- The World Meteorological Organization's Early Warning Systems for Floods (EWS-F) program, supported by USAID Bureau of Humanitarian Assistance, targets 14 Small Island Developing States across multiple regions [WMO, 2024].

**Water Resource Management in Refugee Settings**
- As of 2023, UNHCR deployed 1,200 smart water sensors in refugee camps, with 40% installed across 10 countries, mainly in Africa [UNHCR, 2023].
- These sensors monitor groundwater resources and support rapid identification of leaks and technical issues, minimizing water wastage.
- UNHCR's refugee operations provided an average of 18 liters of water per person per day, slightly below the minimum standard of 20 liters for protracted situations [UNHCR Global Report, 2023].
- Refugee settlements face increasing pressure on water supply and irrigation systems as populations swell, particularly in regions like Ethiopia's Somali Region hosting hundreds of thousands of refugees [IWMI, 2024].

**Climate Adaptation and Water Security**
- The water footprint of refugee displacements (~31 km³ annually in 2016) is disproportionately carried by countries already facing water scarcity conditions [Nature Communications, 2023].
- Climate change impacts on water quantity and quality require better understanding of hydrological systems, underpinned by science and innovation suitable for complex interrelated water challenges [UNESCO IHP, 2024].
- Droughts are a primary driver of humanitarian crises in arid regions, yet early warning systems that index humanitarian financing often omit water security data in favor of food security monitoring [PMC, 2024].

**Disaster Response and Infrastructure Assessment**
- Water Mission's Disaster Assistance Response Team (DART) conducts rapid on-the-ground needs assessments including water quality analysis to determine efficient, effective solutions [Water Mission, 2023].
- Engineers monitor water quality and regulate flow during emergency operations to ensure safe water delivery.
- Communities create 'vulnerability maps' to identify areas most at risk of flooding, which inform infrastructure improvement projects with local authorities [WaterAid, 2024].

### 1.2 Transboundary Monitoring Considerations

Transboundary river basins present unique challenges and opportunities:

- Flood risk management is complicated by conflicting objectives between multiple countries, lack of systematic data monitoring and sharing, and limited collaboration in unified hazard prediction systems [ESCAP/RIMES, 2024].
- The Global Flood Awareness System (GloFAS) provides transboundary early flood guidance over medium to large river basins worldwide, developed through cooperation between the European Commission's Joint Research Centre, ECMWF, Red Cross, and research institutions [ECMWF, 2024].
- The Mekong River Basin's Regional Flood Management and Mitigation Centre in Phnom Penh produces accurate regional forecasts with tools for basinwide risk assessment and transboundary impact analysis [MRC, 2024].
- Successful transboundary systems require joint monitoring, forecasting, early warning, coordinated risk assessment, and appropriate legal/institutional frameworks [WMO, 2024].

## 2. Specific Humanitarian Use Cases for Real-Time River Monitoring

### 2.1 Flood Early Warning Systems

**Community-Based Early Warning Systems (CBEWS)**

CBEWS represent a people-centered approach using timely, simple, and low-cost technology to disseminate information to vulnerable downstream communities through networks of communities and government bodies [ICIMOD, 2024].

**Key Characteristics:**
- Detection and analysis using sensors (water-level radars, pressure sensors, rain gauges) with algorithms to predict flood risks
- Information flow from upstream to downstream communities in near real-time
- Community members and government agencies take ownership of system management
- Notifications sent via SMS, email, apps, or public broadcast systems

**Real-World Examples:**

**Nepal - Practical Action (2002-present)**
- Practical Action piloted CBFEWS in the West Rapti basin with the Department of Hydrology and Meteorology
- Installed 11 Automatic Weather Stations in rural municipalities along Melamchi, Indrawati, and Yangri watersheds
- Partnered with mobile operators to ensure all phones in at-risk areas receive warnings regardless of network or residence
- Challenge: Limited lead time (2-3 hours in Karnali River basin) adequate for saving lives but insufficient for assets/livelihoods [Practical Action, 2024]

**Bangladesh - Practical Action (2015-present)**
- Works alongside 40,000 individuals across eight communities in Faridpur
- Developed 'voice SMS' - short voice messages for people who cannot read
- Sends warnings to 10,000 registered mobile phone numbers
- Improved warnings reach up to 40,000 people several days in advance
- Increased flood warning time from mere minutes to several hours through innovative early warning systems and detailed action plans [Practical Action, 2024]

**Uganda - Elegu Flood Plain**
- Community-based transboundary flood early warning system
- Uses most commonly modern automated scientific systems including mass social media, SMS, MMS, and river gauges
- Emphasizes community sustainability and empowerment with systems monitored by community members [Journal of Flood Risk Management, 2025]

**Mexico - Mexican Red Cross**
- Zurich Flood Resilience Alliance produced Flood Resilience Measurement for Communities tool
- Web-based system with 44 indicators to measure community resilience before and after floods
- Focuses on empowering communities and influencing national change [PrepareCenter, 2024]

### 2.2 Water Resource Management

**Smart Monitoring in Refugee Camps**

UNHCR's deployment of smart water sensors represents a significant advancement in refugee camp water management:

- Real-time monitoring of groundwater levels prevents aquifer depletion
- Rapid leak detection minimizes water wastage
- Data-driven decision making for resource allocation
- Long-term monitoring supports sustainable groundwater management in climate-resilient WASH systems [UNHCR, 2023]

**Surface Water Monitoring**

UNHCR assumes all water available during emergencies is contaminated, especially surface water bodies (lakes, ponds, rivers). Monitoring includes:
- Construction of gravel filters and sedimentation tanks
- Routine maintenance of water treatment systems
- Enhanced availability of good quality surface water to persons of concern and host communities [UNHCR Water Manual, 2024]

### 2.3 Disaster Response and Recovery

**Rapid Needs Assessment**

Water Mission's approach demonstrates integration of monitoring into disaster response:
- Rapid on-the-ground needs assessment upon deployment
- Water quality analysis to determine most efficient solutions
- Engineers monitor water quality and regulate flow during operations
- Partnerships with local governments and response organizations [Water Mission, 2023]

**Technology for Remote and Inaccessible Areas**

Satellite remote sensing and emerging technologies support disaster response:
- Cloud to Street has supported humanitarian organizations and governments in 28 African countries with near real-time multi-sensor flood monitoring since 2015
- Uses Landsat and Sentinel satellites with deep learning-based CNN flood detection
- Applied to recent floods in southern Africa for quantitative flood size assessment, guiding humanitarian funding decisions [Cloud to Street, 2024]
- DFO Flood Observatory maintains global atlas particularly for research, humanitarian, and water resources applications [University of Colorado Boulder, 2024]

### 2.4 Climate Adaptation and Resilience Building

**Participatory Monitoring**

Community engagement in water resource monitoring enhances project sustainability:
- World Bank study (2010) of 121 rural water supply projects in 49 countries demonstrated that community involvement at all project stages contributes to effectiveness
- Participatory methods orchestrated by Community-Based Organizations and executing agencies foster sustained engagement
- Citizen science leverages technological advances for more robust, open, democratic decision-making [Applied Water Science, 2020]

**Digital Transformation Initiatives**

IFRC's Digital Transformation Investment Program (DTIP):
- Supported more than 30 National Societies with Microsoft 365 tools, devices, connectivity, security, and digital skill-building
- Aims to add data-driven early warning systems for disaster management
- Codevelops digital transformation roadmaps with National Societies [Microsoft Customer Stories, 2024]

## 3. Challenges Faced by Humanitarian Organizations in Deploying Technical Monitoring Systems

### 3.1 Resource Constraints

**Financial and Human Resource Limitations**

NGOs, especially smaller ones, struggle with limited resources essential for comprehensive monitoring and evaluation systems:
- Comprehensive M&E systems require trained staff, access to technology, and sufficient funding
- Lack of resources—both financial and human—in monitoring efforts is widespread
- Many INGOs lack the knowledge, expertise, or organizational capacity to support effective ICT exploitation for development [Kestria, 2024; SDS Consulting, 2024]

**Implications for OpenRiverCam:**
- System must be low-cost not just in hardware but also in training and ongoing support
- Should minimize need for specialized technical staff
- Must provide clear ROI demonstration for resource-constrained organizations

### 3.2 Infrastructure Challenges

**Power Supply Issues**

Power disruption is a primary reason for depressing ICT adoption in developing countries:
- Power supply interruption is one of the most common challenges
- Solutions include: generators with backup UPS systems, diesel generators, lithium-ion batteries with onboard generators, car batteries with inverters
- Solar-charged power packs sized according to sensor load and regional sunlight conditions enable continuous operation in areas without fixed power [NexSens, 2024; HCI Energy, 2024]

**Connectivity Constraints**

Poor communicational infrastructure significantly hinders ICT application:
- Poor internet bandwidth limits data transfer capabilities
- Larger rural populations in developing countries create additional difficulties in providing technical infrastructure nationally
- Satellite connections are costly, may not be legally permitted, and carry political/surveillance risks
- Alternative solutions needed for offline/low-connectivity environments [Devex, 2024; ICRC Law and Policy, 2024]

**Implications for OpenRiverCam:**
- System should support offline operation with periodic data synchronization
- Consider low-bandwidth data transmission options
- Solar power compatibility essential
- Local data storage capabilities needed

### 3.3 Technical Capacity Gaps

**Skills and Knowledge Deficits**

The digital divide exacerbates existing inequalities:
- NGOs in developing countries may lack access to infrastructure and skills for effective digital tool utilization
- Many participants may not be technically savvy
- Training capacity for technical monitoring systems limited [Climate Sustainability Directory, 2024]

**Organizational Capacity**

Many organizations are not equipped internally to support effective ICT exploitation:
- Simply do not have the knowledge, expertise, or organizational capacity needed
- Over-complicating operating environments with too many systems hinders effectiveness
- Particularly challenging where many participants don't interact face-to-face [Kestria, 2024]

**Implications for OpenRiverCam:**
- System must be intuitive enough for non-technical users
- Training materials must be exceptional quality and accessible
- Local capacity building should be core component
- System should integrate with, not complicate, existing workflows

### 3.4 Data Quality and Management

**Data Scarcity and Quality Issues**

Monitoring in situations of data scarcity is a major challenge:
- Monitoring SDG indicators particularly challenging in many developing countries
- Gathering high-quality data can be daunting for NGOs
- Limited M&E staff and lack of proper documentation systems affect data quality [SDS Consulting, 2024]

**Implications for OpenRiverCam:**
- System should include built-in quality control mechanisms
- Clear guidance on data validation needed
- Simple data management and storage solutions required
- Consider automated data quality checks

### 3.5 Political and Security Constraints

**Operational Restrictions**

Political environments pose significant risks:
- NGOs may face restrictions on operations or scrutiny limiting functionality
- Security concerns around deploying staff in fragile countries push organizations toward third-party monitoring techniques
- Remote management reduces quality assurance opportunities [World Bank IEG, 2024]

**Connectivity Disruptions**

Power shortages due to electricity/fuel scarcity can disrupt connectivity infrastructure in conflict-affected areas:
- Makes remote monitoring especially difficult
- Humanitarian organizations use connectivity for remote documentation of potential violations and real-time interventions
- Disruptions have serious humanitarian consequences [ICRC, 2024]

**Implications for OpenRiverCam:**
- System should be robust in unstable environments
- Consider data security and privacy protections
- Support for intermittent operation and recovery from disruptions needed
- Low profile/non-provocative installation options

### 3.6 System Complexity and Usability

**Over-Complication Challenges**

Complexity hinders adoption and effectiveness:
- Over-complicating operating environments with too many systems reduces effectiveness
- Particularly problematic where participants may not interact face-to-face and aren't technically savvy
- Need for systems that are simple, intuitive, and well-integrated [Kestria, 2024]

**Implications for OpenRiverCam:**
- Simplicity should be design priority
- User interface must be extremely intuitive
- Minimize number of steps required for operation
- Provide clear, visual feedback on system status

## 4. Best Practices for Technical Documentation for Non-Technical Humanitarian Staff

### 4.1 Humanitarian Documentation Standards

**MSF (Médecins Sans Frontières) Approach**

MSF's medical-operational guidelines provide an excellent model for technical documentation in resource-limited humanitarian contexts:

**Key Principles:**
- Based on scientific data AND field experience
- Particularly useful in humanitarian emergencies with limited technical facilities
- Adapts protocols since field means are not those of university hospitals
- Available in multiple languages (Arabic, English, French, Spanish)
- Accessible in both printed and digital versions [MSF, 2024]

**Digital Accessibility:**
- Easily searchable mobile app with latest protocols
- All content downloadable before mission
- Accessible at any time, even offline
- Available at: https://medicalguidelines.msf.org/en

**Content Approach:**
- Practical recommendations for implementation of specific activities in the field
- Draws from WHO and leading medical institutions
- Incorporates published, peer-reviewed scientific literature
- Emphasizes practical application over theory

### 4.2 General Best Practices for Non-Technical Audiences

**Clear and Simple Language**

Multiple sources emphasize the critical importance of accessibility:
- Write using clear and simple language easy to understand for both technical and non-technical users
- Avoid jargon and unnecessary technical terms where possible
- When technical terms are necessary, define them clearly
- For non-technical users, provide clear and easy-to-understand guidance [Invgate Blog, 2024; Scribe, 2024]

**Practical Application:**
- Instead of: "Configure the LSPIV algorithm parameters to optimize particle tracking velocimetry"
- Use: "Adjust the camera settings to clearly see particles moving on the water surface"

**Eliminate or Explain Technical Jargon**

- Developers may be the main reader base, but non-technical employees (product managers, program officers) also visit technical documentation
- When writing for non-technical stakeholders, avoid jargon, provide high-level overviews, and use visuals like flowcharts or diagrams to simplify complex concepts [Whatfix, 2024; Archbee Blog, 2024]

**Visual Elements and Multimedia**

Extensive use of visual aids is consistently recommended:
- Diagrams, screenshots, and other visuals can explain a concept more clearly than text alone
- Adding multimedia like colors, charts, and graphs to clearly display key data points makes documentation more visually appealing and easier to comprehend
- Use flowcharts or diagrams to simplify complex concepts [Scribe, 2024; Paligo, 2024]

**Visual Types to Include:**
- Step-by-step screenshots with annotations
- Flowcharts for decision-making processes
- Diagrams showing equipment setup and installation
- Photos of correct vs. incorrect installations
- Videos demonstrating key procedures
- Infographics summarizing key concepts

### 4.3 Audience-First Approach

**Understanding Your Users**

Before writing anything, clarify who the documentation is for:
- Are they IT professionals, developers, business users, or external customers?
- Successful technical documentation caters to many different types of users, from non-technical to highly experienced
- Consider the specific context: field conditions, stress levels, available resources [Author-it, 2024]

**For Humanitarian Staff Specifically:**
- IT officers with general tech knowledge but not hydrology expertise
- Program officers managing projects without technical backgrounds
- Field staff operating under challenging conditions
- Community members who may have limited formal education
- Staff working in multiple languages and cultural contexts

**User Personas for OpenRiverCam Documentation:**

1. **Field Program Officer**: Manages water/flood monitoring project, no hydrology background, needs to understand what the system does and how to troubleshoot basic issues

2. **Local IT Officer**: Technical background in general IT, responsible for system setup and maintenance, needs detailed installation and configuration guidance

3. **Community Operator**: Local staff member who checks the system daily, needs simple visual guides for routine operations and when to call for help

4. **Humanitarian WASH Specialist**: Water/sanitation expert, understands water issues but not camera technology, needs to interpret data for decision-making

### 4.4 Testing with Users

**Validation is Essential**

Test groups should include technical and non-technical users in a variety of real-life situations [Author-it, 2024]

**Testing Process:**
1. Draft documentation with subject matter experts
2. Review with representative non-technical users
3. Observe users attempting to follow documentation
4. Note points of confusion or error
5. Revise based on feedback
6. Repeat testing cycle
7. Field test in actual deployment conditions

**Global WASH Cluster Training Model**

The Global WASH Cluster demonstrates effective training pathways for humanitarian staff:
- Progressive learning: e-learning → facilitated online → residential training
- Varied time commitments: 2-hour online, 10-hour e-learning, multi-day residential
- Designed specifically for WASH partners and UNICEF staff
- Focuses on practical skills for emergency contexts [Global WASH Cluster, 2024]

### 4.5 Structure and Organization

**Recommended Documentation Structure**

Based on best practices across multiple sources:

**1. Quick Start Guide (1-2 pages)**
- Absolute minimum needed to get system running
- Assumes successful installation already complete
- Large, clear visuals
- Minimal text
- "What to do if something goes wrong" section

**2. Installation and Setup Manual**
- Step-by-step installation procedures
- Equipment list with photos
- Site selection guidance
- Safety considerations
- Troubleshooting common installation issues

**3. User Operations Guide**
- Daily/weekly/monthly operational procedures
- How to check if system is working correctly
- Simple data interpretation guidance
- When to perform maintenance
- Emergency procedures

**4. Technical Reference**
- Detailed technical specifications
- Advanced configuration options
- Data processing algorithms (for technical audiences)
- Integration with other systems
- API documentation if applicable

**5. Troubleshooting and Maintenance**
- Common problems and solutions with photos
- Diagnostic procedures
- Preventive maintenance schedule
- When to call for technical support
- Parts replacement procedures

**6. Training Materials**
- Presentation slides
- Hands-on exercise guides
- Assessment tools
- Train-the-trainer resources

### 4.6 Specific Recommendations for OpenRiverCam

**Hydrology Concepts for Non-Hydrologists**

Key concepts to explain simply:

**Discharge (River Flow)**
- Instead of: "Volumetric discharge rate in cubic meters per second"
- Use: "How much water is flowing past this point - like measuring how many buckets of water go by each second"
- Include visual: Animation or diagram showing water volume passing a point

**Stage-Discharge Relationship (Rating Curve)**
- Instead of: "Empirical relationship between water level and volumetric discharge"
- Use: "As the river gets deeper, more water flows through it. We measure the river depth to estimate how much water is flowing. The rating curve is like a conversion chart that tells us: if the river is this deep, this much water is flowing"
- Include visual: Simple graph showing water level on one axis, flow on the other, with example points marked

**Surface Velocity vs. Average Velocity**
- Instead of: "Surface velocity must be adjusted by a coefficient to estimate depth-averaged velocity"
- Use: "The water on top of the river flows faster than water near the bottom (which is slowed by friction with the riverbed). The camera measures the surface speed. We multiply by 0.85 to estimate the average speed throughout the whole river depth"
- Include visual: Side-view diagram of river with arrows showing velocity differences

**Camera-Based Measurement Principles**
- Instead of: "Large-scale particle image velocimetry using optical flow algorithms"
- Use: "The camera takes a video of the river surface. The software tracks how fast features (like foam, debris, or ripples) move across the water. By knowing how far apart things are in the real world, we can calculate how fast the water is moving"
- Include visual: Before/after images showing tracked features, with arrows indicating movement

**Documentation Format Recommendations**

1. **Field Guide (Waterproof, Pocket-Sized)**
   - Laminated quick reference cards
   - Visual flowcharts for common tasks
   - Minimal text, maximum visuals
   - Multiple languages on same card where possible

2. **Digital Interactive Guide**
   - Offline-capable web application or app
   - Video demonstrations of all key procedures
   - Interactive troubleshooting decision trees
   - Searchable database of FAQs
   - Photo upload capability for requesting remote support

3. **Training Videos**
   - Short (5-10 minute) focused modules
   - Available in multiple languages
   - Downloadable for offline viewing
   - Culturally appropriate for target regions
   - Demonstrating real field conditions, not idealized lab settings

4. **Pictorial Manuals**
   - Inspired by IKEA assembly instructions
   - Step-by-step with one action per page/screen
   - Minimal text reliance
   - Universal symbols and icons
   - Works across literacy levels and languages

## 5. Examples of Successful Low-Cost, Deployable Monitoring Systems Used by Humanitarian Organizations

### 5.1 Camera-Based River Monitoring Systems

**OpenRiverCam**

OpenRiverCam represents the state-of-the-art in accessible camera-based discharge measurement:

**Technical Approach:**
- Fully open-source, user-friendly, low-cost, sustainable web-software stack with API
- Establishes and maintains river rating curves in small to medium-sized streams based on computer vision methods
- Uses Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV), Particle Tracking Velocimetry (PTV), and Dense Optical Flow
- Measures stream velocities and interprets river flow from short movie snapshots [pyOpenRiverCam, 2024]

**Hardware Requirements:**
- Permanent camera providing view of river surface (IP cameras, drones, or smartphones)
- Permanent staff gauge for water level readings
- Occasional bathymetric survey of river's cross section with standard surveying equipment
- Widely available observation platforms [OpenRiverCam EGU, 2021]

**Software Components:**
- pyorc (pyOpenRiverCam): Fully open-source library for image-based river flow analysis
- Implements Large-scale Particle Image Velocimetry using OpenPIV library
- Reprojections and image pre-processing with OpenCV
- Available on GitHub and PyPI [GitHub localdevices/pyorc, 2024]

**Collaborative Development:**
- Co-designed with practitioners from The Netherlands (Waterboard Limburg, KNMI) and Tanzania
- Can be operated with local people, local devices, and open software at any scale
- Leads to job creation and locally sustainable services
- Supported by EU Horizon Europe funding and WMO HydroHub program [LinkedIn, 2024]

**Advantages Over Traditional Methods:**
- Non-contact measurement: camera not in water, safe from floods
- Flexible mounting position
- No need for expensive structures/constructions
- Automated to reduce labor
- Produces 'real-time' discharge measurements
- Correlation studies show good agreement with ADCP measurements [Academia.edu, 2021]

### 5.2 Low-Cost IoT Flood Monitoring Systems

**Arduino-Based Systems**

Multiple humanitarian and research projects have successfully deployed Arduino-based flood monitoring:

**Technical Components:**
- HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensors measuring distance by emitting ultrasonic waves
- Arduino microcontrollers (Wemos D1 ESP8266, Arduino Nano, ESP32)
- SIM900a modules for SMS communication
- Solar-powered systems functioning as stand-alone beacons
- Real-time data transmission via 3G networks and MQTT protocol [ResearchGate, 2024]

**Cost and Deployment Benefits:**
- Low-cost sensors facilitate broader, more cost-effective deployment
- Enhances coverage and effectiveness of flood monitoring networks
- Designed with affordability for widespread deployment
- Scales for extensive monitoring networks [ScienceDirect, 2023]

**Communication Systems:**
- Real-time data updates to IoT platforms: The Things Network, TagoIO, ThingSpeak
- Triggers early flood warnings sent via mobile applications and web-based dashboards
- SMS alerts to authorities and residents
- Empowers emergency managers to plan resource deployment within limited alert time [IRJMETS, 2022]

**Real-World Implementation:**
- Used in flood-prone communities across Asia and Africa
- Practical, cost-effective solutions for resource-limited settings
- IoT presents promise for resolving real-time monitoring challenges through affordable, scalable sensor grids [SSRN, 2021]

### 5.3 SMS-Based Alert Systems

**Voice SMS in Bangladesh - Practical Action**

One of the most successful low-cost alert systems for non-literate populations:

**Technical Approach:**
- Short voice messages instead of text SMS
- Sent to almost 10,000 registered mobile phone numbers
- Accessible and actionable for people who cannot read
- Easily accessed and understood by receivers who can take appropriate action [Zurich Flood Resilience Alliance, 2024]

**Effectiveness:**
- Warnings reach up to 40,000 people
- Delivered several days in advance of potential flooding
- Increased warning time from mere minutes to several hours
- Works alongside communities in eight areas in Faridpur [Practical Action, 2024]

**Why It Works:**
- Most families have access to at least one basic mobile phone
- Simple yet effective warning system
- One of fastest and most cost-effective methods of alerting authorities and vulnerable residents
- Overcomes literacy barriers through audio messaging [ResearchGate, 2024]

**Regional Applications:**

**Laos**: SMS-based flood warning systems address regular major disasters resulting in significant damage to national socioeconomic development and loss of life [ResearchGate, 2016]

**Rwanda**: SMS has potential to be effective community messaging system for synchronized information exchange between authorities and citizens, particularly during emergencies and disasters [Academia.edu, 2014]

**Community Engagement Model:**

- Involved in managing and disseminating early warning information
- Upstream communities disseminate near real-time information to vulnerable downstream communities
- Empowers individuals to act in sufficient time and appropriate manner [ICIMOD, 2024]

## Appendices

### Appendix A: Key Terminology for Humanitarian Staff

**Discharge**: The volume of water flowing through a river cross-section per unit time (typically measured in cubic meters per second or m³/s)

**Stage**: The water level or height in a river (measured in meters or feet above a reference point)

**Rating Curve**: A graph showing the relationship between water level (stage) and discharge - used to estimate discharge from easier-to-measure water level

**LSPIV**: Large-Scale Particle Image Velocimetry - technique using cameras to measure water surface velocity by tracking movement of features

**Bathymetry**: Measurement of the depth and shape of the river channel cross-section

**Gauge**: A device or location for measuring water level or flow

**Real-time Monitoring**: Continuous automated measurement with immediate data availability

**Early Warning System**: Integrated system to detect hazards early and alert at-risk populations with sufficient time to respond

**Community-Based**: System managed by and for local communities rather than only by external experts

**Non-contact Measurement**: Measurement technique that doesn't require instruments in the water

### Appendix B: Sample Quick Start Checklist

**Daily System Health Check (5 minutes)**

□ Camera has power (green light visible)
□ Camera view is clear (no dirt, condensation, or obstruction)
□ Staff gauge is visible in camera view
□ Data updated in last 24 hours (check timestamp)
□ No error messages displayed

1. Take photo of problem
2. Check troubleshooting guide (page XX)

**Weekly Data Quality Check (15 minutes)**

□ Compare camera flow reading to manual observation (does it make sense?)
□ Check for debris on staff gauge - clean if needed
□ Review data for unusual patterns
□ Download backup data file

**Monthly Maintenance (1 hour)**

□ Clean camera lens and housing
□ Check all cable connections
□ Verify battery charge level (if solar powered)
□ Update any software if prompted
□ Test alert system (if configured)
